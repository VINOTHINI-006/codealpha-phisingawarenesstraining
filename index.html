Module 01

What is Phishing?

Understand the fundamentals of one of the world's most prevalent cyber threats.

Definition

Phishing is a cyberattack where criminals impersonate legitimate organisations or individuals via email, SMS, or websites to trick victims into revealing sensitive information like passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

Why It's Dangerous

Phishing attacks bypass expensive technical security controls by targeting the human element. A single successful attack can result in financial loss, identity theft, corporate data breaches, and severe reputational damage.

How Attackers Operate

Attackers craft convincing messages that create urgency, fear, or excitement. They use spoofed domains, cloned websites, and social engineering to manipulate victims into taking actions that compromise their security.

Anatomy of a Phishing Attack

01
Reconnaissance

Attacker gathers target info from social media and public sources.

02
Crafting the Lure

Creates convincing fake email or website mimicking a trusted brand.

03
Delivery

Sends the phishing message via email, SMS, or social media.

04
Exploitation

Victim clicks the link and enters credentials on a fake page.

05
Data Theft

Attacker harvests stolen credentials or installs malware.

Module 02

Types of Phishing

Click each card to learn more about different attack vectors.

Email Phishing

Mass email attacks impersonating trusted brands.

The most common form — attackers send bulk emails mimicking banks, PayPal, Amazon, or government agencies. These emails create urgency ("Your account will be suspended!") and direct users to fake login pages designed to harvest credentials.

Fake sender domain Urgent language Suspicious links

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks on specific individuals.

Unlike mass phishing, spear phishing is tailored to a specific individual using personal information gathered from LinkedIn, social media, or company websites. These attacks are extremely convincing and harder to detect, often targeting executives (whaling).

Personalised content Insider knowledge Executive targeting

Smishing

Phishing delivered through SMS text messages.

SMS phishing (smishing) uses text messages to lure victims. Common examples include fake delivery notifications ("Your parcel is held, click here"), bank fraud alerts, and prize winnings. SMS links are harder to inspect on mobile devices, increasing success rates.

Short URLs Unknown senders Fake OTP requests

Vishing

Voice call phishing using impersonation.

Voice phishing (vishing) uses phone calls where attackers impersonate bank representatives, tech support agents, or government officials. They use caller ID spoofing and psychological pressure to extract sensitive information like PINs, passwords, or social security numbers in real time.

Spoofed caller ID High pressure tactics Authority impersonation

Social Media Phishing

Attacks via Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn & more.

Attackers create fake social media profiles or compromise real accounts to send malicious links. Fake giveaways, cloned login pages, and fraudulent messages from "friends" are common. LinkedIn is particularly targeted for business credential theft and corporate espionage.

Fake profiles Cloned pages Fake giveaways

Whaling

Spear phishing targeting C-suite executives.

Whaling targets high-value individuals — CEOs, CFOs, and board members — using highly convincing, professionally crafted messages. Attackers may pose as legal firms, auditors, or regulatory bodies. The goal is often business email compromise (BEC) or authorising fraudulent wire transfers.

Executive targeting Wire transfer fraud Business Email Compromise

Phishing Email Examples

Hover over highlighted elements to reveal red flags. Can you spot the phishing email?

Legitimate Email
From: no-reply@paypal.com ✓ Verified
To: you@email.com
Subject: Your recent PayPal transaction
Date: Mon, 9 Jun 2025, 10:34 AM

Dear John Smith,

We're confirming your payment of $49.99 to Netflix Inc. sent on 9 June 2025.

If you didn't authorise this transaction, please visit your PayPal dashboard to review activity.

Transaction ID: TXN-2025-88412

Log in to PayPal

Thank you for using PayPal,
The PayPal Team

Why this is legitimate

  • Sender domain matches official paypal.com
  • Professional, calm tone — no urgency
  • Refers to specific transaction details
  • No requests for passwords or sensitive data
  • Link goes to paypal.com (hoverable)
Phishing Email
From: security@paypa1-alert.com
To: you@email.com
Subject: URGENT: Your PayPal Account Has Been Suspended!!
Date: Mon, 9 Jun 2025, 3:12 AM

Dear Valued Customer,

Your account has been SUSPENDED due to suspicious activity!! You must verify your identity within 24 HOURS or your account will be PERMANENTLY DELETED.

Please to click the link below to verify your informations immediately:

http://paypa1-login.ru/verify-account

Please have your Password, Social Security Number, and Credit Card details ready to verify.

PayPal Security Team

Red Flags Detected

  • Lookalike domain: paypa1-alert.com
  • Extreme urgency: "SUSPENDED", "24 HOURS"
  • Generic greeting: "Dear Valued Customer"
  • Grammar mistakes throughout
  • Suspicious .ru domain link
  • Requests for passwords and SSN

What to Look For on a Website

HTTPS Padlock

Always look for the padlock icon and HTTPS in the URL bar. Never enter credentials on HTTP sites.

Domain Spelling

Check every character carefully. Attackers use typosquatting: paypa1.com, amaz0n.com, g00gle.com.

Domain Extension

Unexpected TLDs like .ru, .xyz, .tk, or .info for well-known brands are a major red flag.

Page Design Quality

Poor image quality, missing content, broken CSS, or misaligned elements indicate a hastily cloned fake site.

Module 05

Social Engineering Tactics

Attackers exploit human psychology. Click each tactic to see a real-world example.

Urgency

Creating artificial time pressure to prevent rational thinking.

Real-world Example

"Your iCloud account will be permanently deleted in 2 hours! Click here immediately to verify your identity and prevent data loss."

Why it works: Urgency bypasses careful thinking. When we feel rushed, we skip verification steps and act on instinct.

Fear

Threatening negative consequences to force immediate action.

Real-world Example

"We have detected malware on your device. Your personal files and banking details are at risk. Call our support team NOW to remove it."

Why it works: Fear of loss triggers a fight-or-flight response, making victims ignore warning signs and comply immediately.

Authority

Impersonating trusted figures to gain unquestioned compliance.

Real-world Example

"This is IRS Criminal Investigations. Your tax returns show fraudulent activity. You must purchase iTunes gift cards now or face immediate arrest."

Why it works: We're trained to obey authority figures like government officials, police, or company executives without question.

Curiosity

Exploiting human inquisitiveness with enticing content.

Real-world Example

"[5 Coworkers Viewed Your Profile] See who checked your LinkedIn. Click here to see who's been looking at you this week."

Why it works: Natural curiosity overrides caution. The promise of exclusive or hidden information is almost irresistible.

Greed

Promising rewards, prizes, or financial gain to lure victims.

Real-world Example

"Congratulations! You've been selected for a $1,000 Amazon gift card. You are our 1,000,000th visitor! Claim your prize in the next 5 minutes."

Why it works: The prospect of easy money or free prizes short-circuits critical thinking, especially when combined with urgency.

Trust

Leveraging existing relationships and familiar brands.

Real-world Example

"Hi, it's Sarah from IT. I'm working on a security audit. Could you send me your login credentials? I'll reset them for you right after."

Why it works: We let our guard down with familiar people and brands. Attackers exploit this by cloning identities of friends, colleagues, or trusted companies.

Module 06

Real-World Case Studies

Learn from real phishing scenarios to recognise them in the wild.

Case Study 1

Fake Bank Email Attack

Scenario: An attacker sends an email pretending to be from Barclays Bank, claiming suspicious activity was detected and the account is temporarily locked.

Target: General banking customers
Method: Spoofed email + fake banking login page
Outcome: Victims enter credentials on cloned site; accounts drained
Lesson: Banks never ask you to verify credentials via email. Always navigate directly to your bank's website by typing the URL manually.
Case Study 2

Fake Delivery Notification

Scenario: A text message arrives claiming a DHL/FedEx parcel is on hold. The victim must pay a "customs fee" of £1.99 to release it.

Target: Online shoppers
Method: Smishing + fake payment page to harvest card details
Outcome: Card details stolen; used for large fraudulent purchases
Lesson: Track deliveries only through official carrier apps. Never pay unexpected fees via links sent in unsolicited texts.
Case Study 3

Fake Job Offer Scam

Scenario: A LinkedIn message from a "Google recruiter" offers a high-paying remote role. The victim is asked to complete a skills assessment via a Google Forms link that requests personal data.

Target: Job seekers on LinkedIn
Method: Fake recruiter profile + credential phishing form
Outcome: Personal/financial info stolen; used for identity fraud
Lesson: Verify recruiters through official company HR channels. Legitimate employers never ask for bank details, SSN, or passwords during initial screening.
Module 07

Cyber Safety Best Practices

Adopt these habits to protect yourself from phishing attacks.

Verify Sender Identity

Always check the full email address, not just the display name. Attackers can set any display name — the domain is what matters.

Hover Before You Click

On desktop, hover over any link to see the real destination URL in the status bar. On mobile, long-press to preview links before opening.

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication

MFA adds a critical second layer of security. Even if your password is stolen, attackers cannot access your account without the second factor.

Use Strong, Unique Passwords

Use a password manager to generate and store unique 16+ character passwords for every account. Never reuse passwords across services.

Keep Software Updated

Install OS and application updates promptly. Phishing attacks frequently exploit known vulnerabilities in outdated software to deliver malware.

Report Suspicious Emails

Use your email client's "Report Phishing" feature. Reporting helps protect your entire organisation and improves spam filters for everyone.

Use Antivirus Software

Maintain active, updated antivirus and anti-malware protection. Modern security suites include phishing link detection and real-time web filtering.

Stay Educated

Phishing tactics evolve constantly. Regularly complete security awareness training and stay informed about new threats and attack techniques.

Module 08

Clicked a Phishing Link? Act Fast.

Don't panic. Follow this incident response flowchart immediately.

01

Disconnect Immediately

Turn off Wi-Fi and disconnect from the internet to stop any ongoing data transmission or malware communication.

CRITICAL — Do this first
02

Change All Passwords

Immediately change passwords for any accounts you used on that device — especially email, banking, and social media.

URGENT — Within 15 minutes
03

Enable MFA Everywhere

Activate multi-factor authentication on all critical accounts to prevent attackers from using any stolen credentials.

URGENT — Within 30 minutes
04

Scan Your Device

Run a full antivirus and anti-malware scan. Check for any newly installed applications or browser extensions you didn't authorise.

IMPORTANT — Within 1 hour
05

Report the Incident

Report to your IT/security team (if at work), your email provider, and relevant authorities. Forward phishing emails to reportphishing@apwg.org.

IMPORTANT — Within 24 hours
06

Contact IT Support

Inform your IT security team with full details of the incident. They can investigate, contain the threat, and help prevent wider organisational compromise.

FOLLOW-UP — As soon as possible
